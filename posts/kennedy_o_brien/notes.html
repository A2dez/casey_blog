    • As a young man, he had passed some exams and was all set to move to Dublin in a few months’ time to pursue a workaday job in his chosen field. However, Young Kennedy was approached by the owner of O’Callaghan’s Tannery in Limerick, a man who doubled up as mayor of Limerick. This tannery was the last vestige of a once-thriving leather industry in Ireland which had been in decline ever since livestock began being exported safely to Britain in the 1820s.
      • Mr O’Callaghan told Kennedy that he had some immediate work for the lad and that he could head off to Dublin when the time came. The lad must have had a good head for numbers, and a reputation for it too, as he was tasked with calculating the number of hides O’Callaghan would be needing for various tasks. Kennedy O’Brien would stay with O’Callaghan for many years to come and he worked his way up the company.

  • In time he became a Travelling Salesman for O’Callaghan, a job then known as a ‘traveller’ and his role was to sell the tannery’s leather to the shoemakers and shoe repair shops around the entire island of Ireland as partition was yet to be implemented. This gave O’Brien a great appreciation of the needs of the people and how badly employment was needed around the country.

“This sample swatch of leathers connects me to the model town of Portlaw in Co Waterford.The factory & town was set up by the Malcolmson family to employ & house their cotton workers circa 1840, the factory was a steel framed structure said to be the largest single span building in the world at that time and turned over 2M of raw cotton and processed 6M yds of calico. After the American war of independance when protectionist tactics were introduced the Irish cotton industry declined and the factory and mill closed. Subsequently the Portlaw Spinning Co. took over the mill but the company had a short life span survive closing in 1904. My families involvement with Portlaw started when my grandfather Kennedy O’Brien set up Irish Leathers in 1920 taking over the factory buildings in Portlaw in 1932 and employing several hundred employees at the height of the tanning industry until the introdution of plastics into shoe making which heralded the downfall of the leather industry in Ireland and the company closed in 1980.

  • My father also called Kennedy O’Brien continued in the leather business diversiving into manufacturing sports shoes and made the running spikes that Ronnie Delaney wore for his 1956 Olympic Medal in Melbourne.

  • I found it facinating as a child to visit Portlaw and the other tanneries my grandfather and his children had an involvement in and

  • spent many hours in the North Fredrick St. factory in Dublin seeing the cutting and forming of leather into shoes instilling in me a ‘can do’ attitude and encouraging my interest in business, and of course my love of interesting leather shoes.

  • This piece of leather from the Irish Tanneries swatch housed in the Portlaw Heritage Centre and who kindly gave me a piece when I visited last Nov 2016, reminds me that my life is made up of lots of happy childhood family memories around leather.”

I met Tom O’Brien at his own home, where we spoke about the day his father started work in a tanning factory in Limerick city, run by the O’Callaghans. He described his father’s early days growing up in the city, and his involvement with the Garryowen Rugby Club in the early 1900s. The family moved to Dublin in 1927, where his father set up business as a wholesale leather merchant under the title of Kennedy O’Brien and Company Ltd (Kennedy was his father’s Christian name, a name which can be traced back in the O’Brien clan). Tom himself began work in his father’s tannery in Milford in 1948.
