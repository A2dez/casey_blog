p175

- The history of leather industries in Ireland during the second half of the nineteenth century and the first quarter of the twentieth was one of decline almost to extinction.
  • 1850s -> small tanneries and shoe making shops were to be found in almost every Irish town

Issues

  • no oak

    • ironworkers had so reduced the supply of timber in Ireland that oak bark was being imported for the tanneries.

  • Export trade in live cattle [also sheep]

  • Tariffs

    • 60% on Irish leather into England but only 10% on English into Ireland.

    • Leather mostly came from West of England tanneries

  • Quality

    • duty on the hides in England

    • duty on the pit in Ireland – thus too many hides through a pit meant poorer quality leather.

  • Demand reduced post famine.

  • No credit to tanneries

  • Leather and Whisky are the two most important branches of manufacture in this country considering the immense amount of labour which is employed in their production
  • F J Maguire 1953, quoted by EJ Riordan “Modern Irish Trade and Industry”, London 1920, p176
    • However, the decline had already begun
  • Why did live cattle get exported?
    • Improvements in sea communications in the late 1820s
  • Irish market less well protected from the competition of imported Leather
  • quality was better in England because duty was levied on the hides but in Ireland it was on the tanning pit, so Irish tanners would try to run as many hides as possible through the same pit, affecting quality.
  • Demand reduced post-famine
  • banks refused to extend sufficient credit to tide tanneries over the crisis

Tariffs

  • 1924: leather was first to have tariffs: boots and shoes

    • with goal of resurrecting tanning industry

    • Brits cut the prices so the tariffs had little effect

Industry non-existent

  • Callaghans in Limerick, 200 hides per week, since 1830

  • in 1928, Tanners federation lodged a case with the Tariff Commission (created in1926)

    • minimal interference (harness leather and manufactured harness) from 1931

  • FF: protective tariffs and import quotas to stimiulate tanning and footwear manufacture

  • post 1935: imports reduced by 75% from 60k hundredweights to 15k in 1939, Domestic production rose from 1932-39: from 18979x12 pairs of femael footwear to 154,246x12, 17 new factores (only 2 in Dublin)

  • Imported Footwear: from 263k dozen pairs to 17k

Boot and shoe factories:

  • clusted in the corridor between Dublin and Newry (Dublin, Drogheda, Dundalk). Different from tanneries

Post-war

  • surge in demand for shoes & boots and leather had to be temporarily imported

General:

  • skill slowed down mechanisation & mass production

  • nature of industry led to small & medium units
